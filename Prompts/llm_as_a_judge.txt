====================================================
VOICE PRESERVATION
==================

Definition:
How well the adapted text maintains the stylistic and communicative character of the original
across four dimensions: professional tone, interpretative framing, domain-specific expertise,
and narrative authority.

Subdimensions:

  Professional Tone Preservation
  Does the adapted text preserve the original writer's voice and communicative character,
  even though it is pitched to a different audience?
  
  Note: Tone WILL shift with more aggressive adaptation strategies — this is expected. 
  However, the adapted text should still feel as though it originates from the same 
  curatorial author. Evaluate whether simplifications and reformulations maintain 
  the writer's characteristic voice: their way of making arguments, their measured pace, 
  their balance of precision and clarity, their institutional presence.
  
  Penalty: the text no longer sounds like the same author; it shifts to enthusiastic, 
  conversational, or pedagogical register foreign to the original; casual language or 
  direct address to the reader that breaks the curatorial voice; loss of the writer's 
  intellectual presence.

  Interpretative Framing Preservation
  Does the adapted text retain the original curatorial viewpoint, thematic connections,
  and interpretive perspective?
  Penalty: reframing the object's significance, adding new interpretive claims, removing
  the original's angle, or altering the narrative the curator constructs around the work.

  Domain-Specific Expertise Preservation
  Does the adapted text maintain the appropriate level of domain formality and specialist
  vocabulary consistent with curatorial discourse, even when simplified for the target
  audience?
  
  Note: The retained vocabulary and technical depth should match the target audience's 
  expertise level. However, the text should still maintain the formal register and 
  disciplinary credibility of museum communication. Over-simplification that sounds 
  informal, amateur, or dismissive of expertise is penalised.
  
  Penalty: removal of domain-specific terms where retention was possible without 
  loss of accessibility; loss of formal register that makes the text sound less 
  authoritative or less disciplinarily grounded; over-simplification that trivializes 
  the subject matter or sounds condescending; informal language inappropriate for 
  institutional voice.

  Narrative Authority Preservation
  Does the adapted text preserve the confident, authoritative assertions of the original,
  or does it introduce unwarranted hedging, added qualifications, or overconfident claims?
  
  Note: Evaluate whether the epistemic stance (the degree of certainty and confidence 
  with which claims are made) matches the original. Penalise both unwarranted hedging 
  (adding "may," "might," "possibly" where the original was definitive) AND inappropriate 
  overconfidence (inserting certainty where the original expressed measured judgment or 
  open interpretation). The adapted text should not claim more expertise or authority 
  than the original warrants.
  
  Penalty: hedging claims that were stated confidently in the original; removing 
  scholarly qualifications that were present; adding certainty or authority not present 
  in the original; editorial commentary not in the source; introducing confidence where 
  the original was appropriately measured or interpretively open; the LLM projecting 
  false authority.

STRUCTURAL PENALTIES (apply to Voice Preservation overall score):
The following structural interventions are inappropriate for curatorial texts regardless of
audience, as they shift institutional prose toward pedagogical or conversational register.
Each one that appears must lower the voice score:
  - Added preamble or introductory sentence not in the original
  - Bold section headers or thematic subheadings inserted into the text
  - Bullet point lists replacing prose
  - Closing "reader note" or engagement prompt (e.g. "Look closely at…")
  - Direct address to the reader ("you", "we", "let's") not present in the original

====================================================
MEANING PRESERVATION
====================

Definition:
How accurately the adapted text conveys the information, concepts, and intended understanding
of the original. Readers of both versions should arrive at substantially the same understanding.

Subdimensions:

  Main Idea Preservation
  Are the central themes and key messages retained?
  Penalty: omission or replacement of central arguments or framings.

  Factual Accuracy
  Are all facts — dates, names, historical details, artistic information — correct?
  Penalty: any factual change, however minor.

  Information Retention
  Are important details and contextual information preserved?
  Penalty: omissions that reduce the reader's understanding of the work or its context.

  Distortion Avoidance
  Does the adaptation avoid introducing misleading interpretations or unsupported claims?
  Penalty: added claims, altered causality, softened or amplified significance not in original.

====================================================
ACCESSIBILITY
=============

Definition:
How well the adapted text improves comprehensibility for its intended audience while maintaining
essential content.

Subdimensions:

  Language Simplicity
  Have complex vocabulary and specialist expressions been replaced with clearer alternatives
  appropriate for the target audience?

  Terminology Accessibility
  Are domain-specific and art-historical terms explained, glossed, or simplified appropriately
  for the target audience's level of expertise?

  Sentence Clarity
  Is sentence structure simplified and organisation easier to follow?

  Audience Appropriateness
  Does the text match the linguistic ability and background knowledge of the intended audience?

IMPORTANT — AUDIENCE-RELATIVE EVALUATION:
Accessibility must be evaluated relative to the intended audience profile, not in absolute terms.
A text adapted for expert non-native speakers (B2–C1) is NOT expected to read like a text for
teenagers, and vice versa. What constitutes appropriate simplification for one audience may
constitute over-simplification or under-simplification for another. Inappropriate simplification
relative to the target audience is penalised under Audience Appropriateness.
